When I was at university, I took a course on AI. It’s not what you’d think. It’s wasn’t what _we_ were expecting. There was some discussion around the more philosophical aspects, such as “What is thought?”, “What is intelligence?” and “Can machines think?” The rest was search algorithms. [Alpha-beta pruning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha–beta_pruning) and such. 

Is that what you think of when someone mentions AI?

There are still familiar concepts. The main one is the constant movement of goalposts. And the suggestion that machines _can’t_ think, that there’s something special about the way that humans process information[^special]. 

At the time, computers certainly didn’t think or operate like humans. [Expert systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert_system) and thousands of lines of [Haskell](https://www.haskell.org) didn’t result in anything terribly usable, even if it was impressive at the time.

One of the main research areas at my university was “natural language processing,” the idea that computers should be able to understand and process human languages rather than have us learn how to talk to them. I remember the lecturer telling us that the computer would need to fully understand the world to allow it to translate between languages, for example. To be able to do the best job, the translator, whether human or machine, has to _understand_ the underlying concepts rather than just pattern match on a word.

In that sense, AI has been A Thing for pretty much as long as there have been computers. In the last decade or so, we got another processing grunt for machine learning to become mainstream. And in the last few years we have large language models, which is what most people mean when we say AI these days.

Despite doing this stuff for decades, I’m still excited by the possibilities of new tech waves. I try to evaluate the potential rather than just what’s there. This requires carefully studying what’s _currently_ possible and trying to disambiguate between truth and hype and potential. Some claims, while not true, will be possible in a few years time[^timelines]. Other claims can never be true. I can’t profess any unique insight, but if you’ve been sitting on the fence it might be worth investing a little time.

The previous bubble was blockchain. It always struck me as a neat idea but I had a hard time seeing real applications. Most current usage is indistinguishable from a scam.

AI -- or more accurately LLMs -- do not, I think, fall into the same category. It’s still early days but they’re already doing quite impressive, quite useful tasks. I have a series of posts coming in the next few weeks discussing this in more detail. The pros and cons, and risks and potential.

[^special]: This is just another case of humans wanting to put ourselves in some special category again. The brain is complex and there are things that we can’t explain, but there’s no magic going on. I see no reason why a sufficiently advanced computer couldn’t do the same thing, albeit likely using different methods. 

[^timelines]: Timelines are difficult to predict, of course. People have been forecasting the availability of self-driving cars for a long time. So far that’s been less bullish and more bullshit. But I don’t see anything _impossible_, just very difficult.
